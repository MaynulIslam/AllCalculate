How Salary-to-Hourly Conversion Works

Converting a salary to an hourly rate depends on how many hours you actually work each year. The standard assumption is 40 hours/week for 52 weeks = 2,080 hours per year, but this changes based on your vacation days and actual work schedule.

The Conversion Formula

Effective Annual Hours = (Work Weeks × Hours Per Week) Work Weeks = 52 − (Vacation Days ÷ Days Per Week) Hourly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ Effective Annual Hours Daily Rate = Hourly Rate × Hours Per Day Weekly Rate = Hourly Rate × Hours Per Week Monthly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ 12 Biweekly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ 26

Understanding Pay Periods

  • Annual: Your full-year compensation (salary + bonuses if applicable).
  • Monthly: Annual salary divided by 12. Note: some months have more working days than others.
  • Biweekly (every 2 weeks): Annual salary divided by 26. This is the most common pay period in the US. You receive 26 paychecks per year, meaning 2 months per year you receive 3 paychecks.
  • Weekly: Annual salary divided by 52.
  • Daily: Your hourly rate times the hours you work per day.
  • Hourly: The base unit — what you earn for each hour worked.

The Importance of Benefits in Total Compensation

Your hourly rate is only part of your total compensation. Benefits can add 20–40% to your cost to employer. When comparing a salaried position to a contract/hourly role, factor in:

  • Health insurance: Employer-sponsored health coverage can be worth $5,000–$20,000/year.
  • Retirement contributions: A 401(k) match of 4–6% of salary is significant.
  • Paid time off: If you have 10 paid vacation days, you're effectively earning that time as compensation.
  • Social Security & Medicare: Employers contribute 7.65% of your wages.
  • Other perks: Dental, vision, life insurance, commuter benefits, stock options.

Overtime Considerations

In the US, non-exempt employees are entitled to overtime pay (1.5× the regular rate) for hours worked beyond 40 per week under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA). Salaried exempt employees are not eligible for overtime regardless of hours worked. If you regularly work more than 40 hours, factor this into your effective hourly rate comparison.

Contractor vs. Employee Rate

As a contractor or freelancer, your quoted hourly rate should be higher than an equivalent salaried employee's hourly rate because you cover your own self-employment taxes (15.3%), benefits, paid time off, and business expenses. A common rule of thumb: multiply a salaried employee's hourly equivalent by 1.5–2× to arrive at a fair contractor rate.
