How Federal Income Tax Brackets Work

The United States uses a progressive tax system, meaning different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. You do not pay your marginal (highest) rate on all your income — only on the income that falls within each bracket.

Example: Single filer with $60,000 taxable income

The first $11,600 is taxed at 10% ($1,160). Income from $11,601 to $47,150 is taxed at 12% ($4,266). Income from $47,151 to $60,000 is taxed at 22% ($2,827). Total tax = $8,253.

Standard vs. Itemized Deductions

The standard deduction is a flat amount you can subtract from your income without needing to track individual expenses. For 2024, it is $14,600 (Single), $29,200 (Married Filing Jointly), $14,600 (Married Filing Separately), or $21,900 (Head of Household).

Itemized deductions allow you to deduct specific expenses such as mortgage interest, state and local taxes (SALT, capped at $10,000), charitable contributions, and large medical expenses. Itemizing is beneficial when your total itemized deductions exceed your standard deduction.

Tips for Reducing Your Tax Bill

  • Maximize contributions to pre-tax retirement accounts (401k, Traditional IRA, SEP-IRA)
  • Contribute to an HSA if you have a high-deductible health plan
  • Claim all eligible deductions and credits
  • Consider tax-loss harvesting on investments
  • Consult a tax professional for personalized advice
Taxable Income Formula:
Taxable Income = Gross Income − Pre-Tax Deductions − Deduction (Standard or Itemized)

Federal Tax: Apply progressive brackets to taxable income.
